💡 Travel Tips

Everything you need to know before you go

🚌 Getting Around ▼
✨

All of your transportation is taken care of — private transfers, buses, and drivers are arranged for the entire trip. Just show up and enjoy the ride.

Driving

Scots drive on the left side of the road. You're not driving, but good to know when crossing streets — look right first!

Tipping

Tipping is appreciated but less expected than in the US. A 10–15% tip at restaurants is generous and very well received. Tips are not expected at bars when ordering drinks. A few pounds for hotel porters is always a nice touch.

Distances

Scotland is smaller than you think — Edinburgh to Loch Lomond is only about 1.5 hours. Edinburgh to Stirling is under an hour. Everything on our itinerary is very manageable.

💷 Money & Practicalities ▼
Currency

Scotland uses British pounds sterling (£), not Euros. Don't show up with Euros expecting them to be accepted — they won't be.

Cards

Credit and debit cards are accepted almost everywhere. Notify your bank before you travel so your card isn't flagged. Always carry a backup card.

Cash

Worth having some British pounds on hand for tips, market stalls on the Royal Mile, and smaller spots that prefer cash. ATMs (called "cashpoints" in Scotland) are widely available.

UK Plug Adapter

The UK uses Type G plugs — three rectangular pins. This is different from US, European, and most other plugs. You need one per device. Do not forget this.

VAT Refund

As a visitor, you may be eligible for a VAT refund on purchases over £30. Ask shops on Buchanan Street for a VAT refund form — it's worth it on cashmere and larger purchases.

📱 Staying Connected ▼
International Roaming

Enable international roaming with your carrier before you leave home — or pick up a local UK SIM card at Edinburgh Airport on arrival. Either way, sort it before you leave the airport.

WhatsApp

WhatsApp is how everyone communicates in the UK. Make sure it's downloaded and set up before you arrive — this is how the group will stay in touch throughout the trip.

WiFi

Both The Balmoral and Cameron House have excellent WiFi. Most restaurants and venues do too. You won't be disconnected.

Emergency Number

The emergency number in Scotland (and all of the UK) is 999 — not 911. Save it in your phone just in case.

🌦️ Weather in June ▼
🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿

There is a saying in Scotland: "If you don't like the weather, wait five minutes." June can give you brilliant sunshine, sideways rain, and cool mist all before lunch. This is not an exaggeration.

Temperature

June temperatures typically range from 50°F–65°F (10°C–18°C). It feels warmer in the sun and significantly cooler near the water or in the wind. Never assume it will be warm.

Layering

The key to Scottish weather is layers. Something you can add and remove easily throughout the day. A light base, a warm mid-layer, and a waterproof outer layer covers every scenario.

Daylight

June is the best month for daylight in Scotland — it stays light until nearly 10:30pm. The long evenings are truly magical, especially at Loch Lomond.

Rain

Pack your windproof umbrella and your waterproof jacket. You may not need them — but you'll be very happy you have them if you do.

Whisky

Scotland produces some of the world's finest whisky (spelled without an 'e' — that's the Irish way). You'll experience it at the Sköll & Hati Norse Bar on Day 2. Don't be afraid to ask for recommendations — bartenders in Scotland take whisky very seriously and love talking about it.

The Scottish Breakfast

A Scottish breakfast is a serious event. Expect eggs, smoked salmon, tattie scones, grilled tomatoes, mushrooms, and fresh pastries. It will keep you going all day — pace yourself.

Dietary Note

When in doubt at a restaurant, ask about fish and vegetarian options — Scotland's coastal access means the fish is always fresh and outstanding. All our restaurants are aware of our dietary restrictions.

🦟 The Midges — Scotland's Tiny Nemesis ▼
⚠️

Midges are Scotland's tiny biting insects and they are the one thing every visitor wishes someone had warned them about. Consider yourself warned.

What Are They?

Midges are extremely small flying insects — barely visible — that bite and swarm in clouds near water and vegetation. They are most active in June, particularly in the morning and evening, and especially near lochs and parks like Pollok Country Park and Loch Lomond.

Smidge

The best repellent is a Scottish brand called Smidge. It's specifically formulated for Scottish midges and works far better than standard DEET sprays. You can order it before the trip or find it in pharmacies in Edinburgh.

When to Watch Out

Midges love calm, overcast, humid conditions — peak midge weather. On breezy or sunny days they're much less of a problem. Keep Smidge in your bag during Pollok Country Park and anytime you're outdoors near Loch Lomond.

🛍️ Shopping ▼
The Royal Mile, Edinburgh

Edinburgh's famous mile-long stretch from the castle to Holyrood Palace is lined with shops. Great for tartan souvenirs, Scottish whisky, shortbread, cashmere, and one-of-a-kind gifts. A mix of tourist shops and genuine local gems — look beyond the obvious.

Buchanan Street, Glasgow

Glasgow's premier shopping street is significantly more upscale than the Royal Mile. Think designer boutiques, high street fashion, and the beautiful Princes Square mall. This is where you'll find the cashmere and luxury Scottish goods worth splurging on.

What to Buy

Scottish cashmere is world-renowned and worth every penny. Tartan and tweed make beautiful gifts. A bottle of single malt whisky. Shortbread. And anything with a thistle on it that makes you smile.

Bags

Plastic bags cost extra in Scotland (10p–25p). Bring your reusable tote — you've already packed it!

💃 What to Expect at a Ceilidh ▼
🎶

A ceilidh (pronounced "KAY-lee") is Scotland's beloved tradition of communal dancing to live folk music. No experience is needed and no one is watching — everyone is in the same boat. It is one of the most joyful, sweaty, laugh-out-loud experiences Scotland has to offer.

The Caller

Every ceilidh has a "caller" — someone who explains and calls out each dance step by step before the music starts. You will be taught everything before you have to do it. No prior knowledge needed whatsoever.

The Music

Expect live fiddles, accordion, and sometimes bagpipes. The music is fast, joyful, and completely infectious. You will not be able to stand still.

The Dances

Classic dances you'll likely encounter:

Strip the Willow

Fast, spinning, and chaotic in the best way. Hold on tight.

The Gay Gordons

Classic partner dance — simple to follow and a great opener.

Dashing White Sergeant

Done in threes — lots of spinning and changing partners.

The Flying Scotsman

One of the fastest and most exhilarating. Pure chaos. Pure fun.

What to Wear

You will work up a sweat — this is guaranteed. Wear something you can move freely in and comfortable flat shoes. The Edinburgh Ceilidh Club floor has seen enough stiletto casualties.

The Golden Rule

Smile, go with it, and don't take yourself too seriously. The whole point is to laugh, spin, and have the time of your life.

🗣️ Scottish Greetings & Phrases ▼
😄

Scots speak English — but with a vocabulary all their own. A few of these will go a long way and the locals will absolutely love that you tried.

"Alright?" Hello / How are you? The correct response is "Alright?" back — not an explanation of how you're actually doing. Americans always get this one wrong 😄
"Cheers" Thank you / You're welcome / Goodbye / Toast Used constantly for everything. When in doubt, say "cheers" — it's always appropriate
"Hiya" / "Hiya hen" Hi / Hi sweetheart "Hen" is an affectionate term for a woman — warm and friendly, not rude at all
"Aye" Yes The most Scottish word there is. Use it freely
"Nae" No As in "nae bother" — no problem
"Wee" Small / little Used constantly — "a wee drink," "a wee walk," "just a wee bit 40" 😉
"Grand" Great / wonderful "That's grand!" is about the highest compliment you'll receive
"Brilliant" Excellent / awesome The Scottish/British equivalent of "awesome." Use it and watch locals smile
"Braw" Beautiful / lovely As in "what a braw day!" — use this one and you'll sound like a local
"Bonnie" Beautiful "Bonnie lass" means beautiful woman. As in the "bonnie banks" of Loch Lomond
"Dinnae fash" Don't worry about it One of our favorites. Very calming energy
"The bill" The check at a restaurant Never say "check please" — ask for "the bill"
"The toilet" / "The loo" The bathroom Never say "bathroom" — they'll think you want to bathe. Ask for the toilet or the loo
"Queue" A line As in "join the queue." Cutting the queue is considered extremely rude — even more than in the US
"I'm away" I'm leaving / I'm off Said as a casual goodbye — "right, I'm away!"
"Ta" Thanks Quick, casual thank you — used constantly in everyday interactions
